# Need Brief: A Personal System for Bridging the Gap Between Intention and Action

## 1. Background

The user is a high-functioning person with strong capabilities and meaningful ambitions. He generally understands what matters to him and what he should do, yet consistently struggles to move from planning into execution.

The problem is not a lack of understanding, intelligence, or planning ability. On the contrary, the user can build plans, set goals, identify priorities, and define desired actions.

The breakdown happens when it is time to begin.

At that moment, several experiences appear together:

- A sense of heaviness and low energy.
- Little or no desire to start.
- Concern that the effort will not lead to success.
- Fear that failure at the task will prove personal failure.
- A desire to protect the limited time, freedom, and energy that remain.
- A rapid shift toward activities that provide immediate relief, such as scrolling on the phone or watching television.

After avoidance, harsh self-criticism appears. This reinforces the sense of failure and makes the next action even harder.

## 2. The Core Problem

The user lives inside a recurring conflict between two internal systems.

### The Planning System

This system thinks long term. It wants progress, achievement, initiative, personal development, professional growth, and a life that reflects the user’s perceived potential.

### The Present-Moment Response System

This system seeks immediate relief from effort, demands, and pressure. At the moment of execution, it says:

- I do not have the energy.
- It is better to rest now.
- The effort is not worth the energy.
- I may not succeed.
- I can do it later.

During planning, the first system is dominant. During execution, the second system takes over. Afterwards, the first system returns as a judge and punishes the user for avoiding the task.

This creates the following cycle:

**Aspiration → Planning → Moment of execution → Heaviness and fear → Avoidance → Temporary relief → Numbness and guilt → Self-criticism → New plan → Further avoidance**

## 3. The Deeper Need

The need is not merely task management.

The user needs a system that helps manage the interaction between:

- Long-term intentions.
- Fluctuating energy.
- Perceived overload.
- Fear of failure.
- A need for autonomy.
- Work, family, and personal responsibilities.
- A tendency toward avoidance and immediate relief.
- Self-criticism after non-completion.

The system should help the user act even when motivation is low, without turning every action into a test of personal worth.

## 4. User Profile

The primary user is someone who:

- Functions at work and at home, but pays a high internal cost.
- Completes many responsibilities while feeling resentment, resistance, or delay.
- Experiences each additional request as consuming the little freedom that remains.
- Tends to rest or escape into screens, yet does not feel restored afterwards.
- Struggles to distinguish genuine rest from avoidance.
- Sets goals but does not follow through at the times he defined.
- Experiences small tasks as large tests.
- Interprets non-completion as a character flaw rather than a behavioral event.
- Compares himself with people who appear more driven, entrepreneurial, successful, or ambitious.
- Struggles to distinguish what genuinely matters to him from what he believes should matter to him.
- May not view career as a central source of passion, but still wants to function reliably and feel in control.

## 5. Jobs to Be Done

### When I Plan a Week

I want to see clearly what truly matters this week, so that I do not create an impossible list and lose myself among dozens of tasks.

### When I Plan a Day

I want to choose from a defined pool of commitments and actions, so that I can build a realistic day based on my available time and energy.

### When the Time to Act Arrives

I want the system to help me begin without reopening the entire debate about whether to do it, when to do it, or whether I have enough energy.

### When I Feel Heavy or Avoidant

I want to identify avoidance in real time and receive a clear way to return to action, without attacking myself and without creating a new plan.

### When I Miss Something

I want to know how to continue from the current point, without feeling that the whole week is ruined and without trying to compensate through overloading.

### When I Finish a Week

I want to understand what actually happened:

- What I completed.
- What I avoided.
- When starting was difficult.
- Which actions improved my energy.
- What was overly ambitious.
- Which goals should move into the following week.
- What should change in my behavior or planning, rather than in my identity.

## 6. Key Pain Points

### Cognitive Overload

Planning requires too many decisions. When the user must decide again what to do, when to do it, and how much to do, avoidance becomes more likely.

### Grandiose Plans

The user is drawn to large, comprehensive plans because they create a temporary sense of control and hope. However, an overly ambitious plan can quickly become another source of failure.

### Difficulty Moving From Planning to Action

Planning itself feels relatively easy. Execution activates heaviness, resistance, and fear.

### Escape Toward Immediate Reward

When a task feels heavy, the user moves toward an easy, available activity, especially screen use. This reduces tension briefly but increases numbness, guilt, and low energy later.

### A Sense of Coercion

Even self-chosen tasks can feel compulsory at the moment of execution.

Requests from others may produce immediate anger because they are experienced as another loss of freedom.

### Disproportionate Self-Criticism

The internal system does not say:

“I did not complete the task.”

It says:

“I am lazy, stupid, mediocre, and a failure.”

This interpretation does not improve performance. It increases fear, exhaustion, and avoidance.

### Difficulty Distinguishing Rest From Avoidance

The user needs rest and periods in which nothing is demanded from him, but much of his free time turns into unplanned scrolling or viewing that does not restore him.

### Lack of Continuity Between Weeks

Goals and insights tend to disappear from one week to the next. Each week can begin from zero without preserving what was learned before.

## 7. What the System Should Enable

The system should help the user:

1. Reduce the gap between intention and execution.
2. Convert broad goals into actions placed in time.
3. Build a realistic week from fixed and variable commitments.
4. Protect work time, personal time, family time, and rest.
5. Preserve a sense of choice within responsibilities.
6. Reduce the number of decisions required in the moment.
7. Distinguish restorative rest from avoidance.
8. Identify patterns of escape into screens.
9. Learn which types of activities improve or reduce energy.
10. Continue after a missed action without resetting the entire process.
11. Measure behavior rather than personal worth.
12. Carry goals, insights, and commitments from one week to the next.
13. Hold work, personal life, family, and health in one system.
14. Give the user an accurate picture of actual behavior rather than the story told by the inner critic.

## 8. Product Principles

### This Is Not a Conventional Productivity System

The problem is not merely how to accomplish more. A system that glorifies output may intensify the difficulty.

The product should treat execution, energy, avoidance, autonomy, and self-criticism as one connected system.

### Fewer Decisions in the Moment

The more internal negotiation required at the moment of action, the more likely avoidance becomes.

### Flexibility Within Structure

The system should allow changes to time, movement of actions between days, and adaptation to reality without turning every change into a full rebuild of the week.

### Action Before Motivation

The product must not assume that the user will act only when motivated. It should support beginning even when desire is low.

### Progress Is Not Perfection

Success metrics should reward returning to the plan, beginning, meeting core commitments, and learning—not only perfect completion.

### A Miss Is Data

A missed action is not a system failure and not a reason to reset. It is information about load, planning, energy, or avoidance.

### Distinguish Obligation From Choice

The system should help the user understand:

- What is mandatory.
- What is optional.
- What is a personal goal.
- What comes from social expectation or comparison.
- What can be delayed.
- What is not important enough.

### Preserve Autonomy

Even when responsibilities exist, the user should feel that he has some choice over when, how, and in what order to perform them.

### Non-Judgmental Language

The product should not use language of laziness, failure, guilt, or punishment.

It should use language such as:

- Completed.
- Not completed.
- Deferred.
- Moved.
- Overloaded.
- Avoidance occurred.
- Adjustment needed.
- Something was learned.

## 9. Core Situations the Product Must Support

### Weekly Planning

The user defines a limited number of weekly goals and builds the week from types of actions he intends to complete.

### Daily Planning

The user chooses which actions to perform today, at what times, and what outcome is expected from each action.

### Immediately Before Starting

The system provides clear context:

- What the action is.
- What counts as completion.
- How long it should take.
- Why it is placed on this day.
- What the first action is.

### During Avoidance

The system allows the user to identify that escape or delay occurred and choose the next response from a limited set of options.

### Changing the Plan

The user can move an action, shorten it, change the expected output, or replace it, but the change remains visible as data rather than erasing the history.

### End of Day

The user sees a short picture of what was completed, what was deferred, and what moved to another day.

### End of Week

The system summarizes the week and helps the user define three clear goals for the next week.

Those goals should appear as context in the following week rather than disappear into an archive.

## 10. What Success Looks Like for the User

The product is successful when the user:

- Starts more of the actions he planned.
- Reduces automatic escape into screens.
- Recovers more quickly after missing something.
- Replans less often.
- Feels less threatened by every request or task.
- Understands more clearly which tasks genuinely matter.
- Integrates work, family, rest, and personal life more effectively.
- Feels that he has choice rather than being driven only by demands.
- Ends a week with information rather than a verdict about himself.
- Gradually develops trust in his ability to decide on an action and follow through.

## 11. Possible Success Metrics

### Behavioral Metrics

- Percentage of planned actions started on time or within a reasonable window.
- Percentage of actions completed.
- Number of actions moved repeatedly.
- Number of screen-escape events.
- Average time between the planned start and the actual start.
- Number of consecutive weeks with a completed weekly review.
- Percentage of weekly goals represented by concrete actions on the schedule.
- Number of times the user returns to action after a missed block.

### Experience Metrics

- Perceived control over the week.
- Perceived overload at the beginning of the day.
- Strength of resistance before an action.
- Level of guilt after non-completion.
- Energy before and after different types of actions.
- Degree of restoration after rest.
- Sense of presence during family time.
- Trust in the ability to follow through on plans.

## 12. What the System Is Not Intended to Be

- It is not a tool designed to maximize working hours.
- It is not an endless task list.
- It is not a system that punishes non-completion.
- It is not a replacement for medical or psychological treatment.
- It should not diagnose depression, ADHD, burnout, or any other condition.
- It should not assume that every problem can be solved through discipline.
- It should not turn every free minute into a task.
- It should not create competition between the user and himself.
- It should not treat all goals as equally important.
- It should not encourage overloading as proof of seriousness.

## 13. Assumptions That Need Validation

1. Does the user need a rigid structure or a flexible structure with clear boundaries?
2. How many weekly actions can be displayed before the system itself becomes overwhelming?
3. Does scoring increase motivation or become another judgment mechanism?
4. Is tracking screen escape useful, or does it create more guilt?
5. Should planning be based on exact times or broad parts of the day?
6. Should obligations, meaningful actions, and restorative actions be separate categories?
7. Should goals be weekly, daily, or hierarchical?
8. How can the system recommend adjustments without reducing the user’s sense of choice?
9. How can the system identify an overly ambitious week before it begins?
10. Should the system learn from patterns and suggest plans based on actual energy?
11. How can the system prevent planning itself from becoming a form of avoidance?
12. How should family responsibilities be represented without framing every request as an interruption?
13. How can rest be protected without becoming unplanned escape?
14. Which data should be stored long term, and which data should not be collected?

## 14. Central Product Question

How might we build a system that helps a capable and ambitious person move from intention to action—even when he is tired, unmotivated, afraid of failure, and experiences demands as a threat to his freedom—without turning the system itself into another source of pressure, judgment, and avoidance?
